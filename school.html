School Gardening

School gardens are more than just plots of land where students grow vegetables. They are powerful tools that can contribute to food security in multiple ways.School gardens can provide fresh, nutritious produce to supplement school feeding programs, especially in areas where access to diverse and healthy food is limited. This is particularly crucial in regions facing food insecurity and malnutrition.
In some cases, students can take home a portion of the harvest, directly contributing to household food availability. This is especially impactful in communities with limited access to markets or where food costs are high.
School gardens offer hands-on learning experiences about healthy eating habits, the importance of fruits and vegetables, and the nutritional value of different foods. This knowledge can lead to long-term dietary changes and improved food choices.

School Gardening Process

  1. Form a garden team
    • Involve teachers, learners, and volunteers.
    • Assign roles: planning, planting, watering, record-keeping.
  2. Select a site
    • Choose a safe, sunny area with access to water.
    • Make sure the soil is not polluted and drains well.
  3. Plan the garden
    • Decide on garden type: vegetable patch, herb garden, or mixed.
    • Design plots in neat rows or raised beds so learners can access easily.
    • Include pathways for movement.
  4. Prepare the soil
    • Clear weeds, litter, and rocks.
    • Loosen the soil and mix in compost or manure.
    • Teach students why healthy soil is important.
  5. Choose crops
    • Select plants suited to the local climate and season.
    • Prefer easy-to-grow crops (spinach, carrots, beans, tomatoes).
    • Pick plants that have educational value (science lessons, nutrition).
  6. Plant the garden
    • Demonstrate how to sow seeds or plant seedlings at the right depth.
    • Label rows with plant names for learning.
    • Involve learners in the process step by step.
  7. Prepare the soil
    • Clear weeds and rocks.
    • Loosen soil with a spade or fork
    • Mix in compost or manure to add nutrients.
  8. Choose seeds or seeding
