Community Gardening

Community gardens are shared spaces where individuals can grow their own fruits, vegetables, and flowers. These gardens are often managed and maintained by local community members, providing a space for people to come together and cultivate their green tombs.The importance of community gardens in a healthy urban environment has been extensively documented, while the garden building involving communities has not been much explored in fast-developing cities. The Community Garden Initiative consisting of an array of progressive actions is formulated by the research team, featuring a process of increasing involvement of community members and decreasing intensity of external interventions.

Key Challenges

  • Lack of Skills and Knowledge
  • Land Acquisition and Tenure
  • Community Engagement and Participation
  • Securing Water Source

Community Gardening Process

  1. Build a community team
    • Gather interested members (neighbors, NGOs, local leaders).
    • Assign roles: coordinator, treasurer, gardener leads, record keepers..
    • Create rules together (who waters, harvests, and shares produce).
  2. Secure land
    • Identify a safe, accessible site (unused land, park area, school grounds).
    • Get permission from the municipality, landowner, or local authority.
    • Ensure there is a reliable water source.
  3. Plan the garden layout
    • Hold meetings with members to design the garden.
    • Decide whether to have individual plots (each family grows their own) or shared plots (everyone contributes and shares harvest).
    • Plan pathways, compost areas, storage space, and possibly fencing.
  4. Prepare the soil
    • Clear the land (weeds, trash, stones).
    • Improve soil with compost, manure, or topsoil.
    • If soil is poor, consider raised beds.
  5. Choose crops
    • Select crops suited to the community’s needs and climate.
    • Mix fast-growing vegetables (like spinach, lettuce) with long-term crops (like maize, pumpkins).
    • Include herbs or medicinal plants if useful to the community.
  6. Planting
    • Follow seasonal calendars (plant warm-season crops in spring/summer, cool-season crops in autumn/winter)
    • Rotate planting tasks so everyone participates.
    • Use labels or charts to track planting dates.
  7. Watering System
    • Set up watering schedules so the responsibility is shared fairly.
    • Use watering cans, hosepipes, or drip irrigation if affordable.
    • Teach water-saving practices (mulching, watering in the morning/evening).
  8. Malching and Weeding
    • Apply mulch (leaves, straw, bark) to conserve moisture.
    • Weed weekly to reduce competition and pests.
    • Organize “weeding days” where members work together.
  9. Pest and Disease Control
    • Monitor plants regularly for insects or disease.
    • Use natural remedies (garlic spray, marigold companion planting).
    • Teach members about safe, chemical-free methods.
  10. Fertilizing and Compost
    • Add compost or organic fertilizer every few weeks.
    • Start a community compost pit using kitchen scraps and garden waste.
    • Use composting as a training activity for members.
  11. Harvesting
    • Decide as a group how to share or sell produce.
    • Rotate who harvests, so it’s fair and transparent.
    • Hold community harvest celebrations to build pride.
  12. Record-keeping and Finances
    • Keep a simple record of crops planted, costs, and harvest amounts.
    • If selling produce, track income and reinvest in tools, seeds, or fencing.
  13. Long-term maintenance
    • Replant new crops each season.
    • Rotate crops to keep soil fertile.
    • Train new members so knowledge is passed on.
  14. Community engagement
    • Organize workshops, school visits, or cooking classes.
    • Involve youth and elders to strengthen community bonds.
    • Partner with local NGOs or businesses for support (seeds, tools, funding).
