Urban-farming

Urban farming has become a potent weapon for improving food security in South Africa. Urban agriculture provides a creative approach to address hunger, unemployment, and nutritional deficiencies given that over 63% of South Africa's people live in urban areas (Stats SA, 2022). By using unused urban land, rooftops, and vertical spaces, communities can produce food closer to the point of consumption, therefore decreasing reliance on big-scale supply chains often interrupted by climate events or economic downturn.
n More and more people are using creative agricultural techniques including hydroponics, aquaponics, and vertical farming. These systems can produce crops year-round irrespective of seasonal change and use up to 90% less water than conventional agriculture. By raising local output, creating jobs, and fostering sustainable urban development, urban farming and creative agri-technology offer realistic, scalable answers to the food security problems facing South Africa.

Urban farming Process

  1. Assess space and resources
    • Identify available space: balconies, rooftops, backyards, or unused lots.
    • Check sunlight, water access, and safety of the area.

    • Rooftop gardens ‚Äì using flat roofs with protective barriers.
    • Hydroponics/aquaponics ‚Äì soil-free systems using water and nutrients.
  3. Plan what to grow
    • Select crops that grow well in limited space (lettuce, spinach, herbs, cherry tomatoes, peppers).
    • Focus on high-value crops (salads, herbs, mushrooms) if selling.
    • Check seasonal planting calendars for your city‚Äôs climate.
  4. Prepare growing medium
    • For soil-based: use lightweight, nutrient-rich soil mixed with compost.
    • For hydroponics: prepare water tanks with nutrient solutions.
    • Ensure good drainage to avoid waterlogging.
  5. Planting
    • Start with seedlings for faster results, or germinate seeds in trays.
    • Use vertical racks or staggered planting to maximize space.
    • Label plants to track growth.
  6. Planting
    • Follow seasonal calendars (plant warm-season crops in spring/summer, cool-season crops in autumn/winter)
    • Rotate planting tasks so everyone participates.
    • Use labels or charts to track planting dates.
  7. Watering System
    • Install drip irrigation or self-watering containers to save water.
    • If hydroponic, monitor water pH and nutrient levels regularly.
    • Water early morning or late afternoon to reduce evaporation.
  8. Lighting & temperature
    • Place plants where they get at least 6 hours of natural sunlight.
    • Use LED grow lights indoors or in shaded areas.
    • Monitor temperature‚Äîurban spaces can get hotter due to concrete.
  9. Pest and Disease Control
    • Use organic methods (neem oil, garlic spray, sticky traps).
    • Encourage natural predators like ladybugs if possible.
    • Keep systems clean, especially in hydroponics.
  10. Fertilizing and Compost
    • Add liquid fertilizer or compost tea regularly.
    • Reuse kitchen waste for compost if space allows.
    • In hydroponics, replenish nutrient solutions weekly.
  11. Harvesting
    • Harvest leafy greens frequently (cut-and-come-again method).
    • Pick vegetables when ripe to encourage more production.
    • For commercial setups, package produce neatly for sale.
  12. Maintenance & scaling
    • Rotate crops to avoid soil exhaustion (if soil-based).
    • Expand vertically or add hydroponic units as demand grows.
    • Train others in the community for collective impact.
  13. Marketing & income (optional)
    • Sell to local markets, restaurants, or directly to households.
    • Highlight ‚Äúfresh, local, and organic‚Äù as selling points.
    • Keep records of costs, yields, and profits for sustainability.
